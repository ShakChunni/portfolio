### 6.2 Ongoing research — BdSL Emergency Triage Translation

**Status:** early-stage research pilot; local codebase and description supplied by Ashfaq. **User-confirmed and locally repository-verified.**

**One-sentence description:**

> An offline, bidirectional Bengali Sign Language translation research project focused on the small, high-value vocabulary needed during emergency medical triage.

**Why it matters:** Deaf sign-language users face communication barriers in emergency medical settings. BdSL remains a comparatively low-resource language, and current literature repeatedly identifies limited data, signer variability, viewpoint variation, and weak real-world generalisation as central problems. A triage-specific pilot narrows the scope to phrases where offline access and latency can matter.

**Current implementation, verified from the supplied description:**

- automated class-wise webcam capture;
- raw-video audit for corrupt, short, dark, or invalid clips;
- MediaPipe Holistic landmark extraction;
- fixed 60-frame sequences with 1,692 features per frame: face, pose, left hand, and right hand landmarks;
- deterministic dataset assembly with validation and provenance manifests;
- a two-layer LSTM baseline with stratified train/validation/test splitting and fixed seeds;
- held-out evaluation, per-class metrics, classification report, confusion matrix, and training curves;
- guards that prevent model training on fewer than two classes and prevent evaluation without a model/test split;
- TFLite export intentionally deferred until the six-class pilot has credible evaluation.

**Current dataset status in the supplied description:** one collected class with 50 clips. This is enough for a pipeline smoke test, not meaningful classification. Confirm whether this number is still current before publishing it.

**What makes the project portfolio-worthy now:** the research discipline, not a result. The audit trail, shape validation, missing-landmark handling, deterministic labels, leakage-aware splits, and refusal to report a single-class “accuracy” show responsible experimental design.

**Research questions to display:**

1. Can a compact landmark-based sequence model recognise a small emergency-triage vocabulary offline with acceptable latency?
2. How well does it generalise across signers, lighting, camera position, handedness, and signing speed?
3. Which landmark groups provide the best accuracy/latency trade-off?
4. How should uncertainty and abstention be surfaced in a safety-sensitive interface?
5. What is required for bidirectional text-to-sign output without reducing BdSL to word-for-word animation?

**Required evaluation before stronger claims:**

- multiple fluent BdSL signers and signer-independent splits;
- class balance and collection protocol documentation;
- accuracy, macro-F1, per-class recall, confusion matrix, and calibration;
- false-positive/false-negative analysis for medically consequential phrases;
- latency, model size, and offline-device benchmarks;
- accessibility review with Deaf/BdSL users and domain review with triage staff;
- consent, governance, retention, and representativeness documentation;
- a visible “not medical advice / not a replacement for an interpreter” boundary.

**Portfolio page modules:** research question, system pipeline, current dataset card, experiment log, failure gallery, ethics/accessibility note, roadmap, and reproducibility notes.

**Naming correction:** the local filename is `bdsm-med-triage-description.md`, but the project is **BdSL**, not “BDSM.” Rename the source file later to avoid an embarrassing public typo.

### 6.3 Ongoing research — Post-quantum cryptography over MQTT under degraded network conditions

**Status:** current research direction supplied by Ashfaq. No paper, repository, authorship list, or results were provided. **User-confirmed topic; all methodological detail below is a proposed framing.**

**Recommended working title:**

> Measuring the Operational Cost of Post-Quantum Security for MQTT Under Degraded Networks

Do not use this as an official paper title without collaborator agreement.

**Research rationale:** MQTT is designed for constrained and bandwidth-limited IoT environments. NIST standardised ML-KEM, ML-DSA, and SLH-DSA in FIPS 203, 204, and 205. Their larger keys, ciphertexts, and signatures make the interaction between post-quantum TLS and lossy, latent, or bandwidth-constrained MQTT links a concrete systems question.

**Strong research question:**

> How do standardised post-quantum and hybrid cryptographic handshakes affect MQTT connection reliability, recovery time, throughput, and device cost as network quality degrades?

**Proposed experiment matrix:**

- Cryptography: classical baseline; ML-KEM; hybrid classical + ML-KEM; ML-DSA and SLH-DSA where authentication design permits.
- MQTT: v5.0; QoS 0, 1, and 2; clean versus persistent sessions; reconnect/resubscribe behaviour; small and large payloads.
- Network: latency, jitter, packet loss, bandwidth cap, reordering, MTU/fragmentation, and intermittent disconnects.
- Devices: desktop baseline plus at least one constrained ARM-class target if available.
- Measures: handshake bytes, packets/fragmentation, success rate, time-to-first-publish, reconnect time, p50/p95 latency, throughput, CPU, peak memory, energy, broker load, and retransmission.
- Reproducibility: containerised broker/client, scripted network emulation, pinned library versions, raw traces, and statistical confidence intervals.

**Portfolio treatment:** show the question, benchmark design, experiment matrix, and weekly findings. Do not post premature winner/loser conclusions. Clearly separate standards facts from experimental observations.

**Context anchors:** NIST FIPS 203/204/205 and the OASIS MQTT 5.0 standard. NIST selected HQC for future standardisation in March 2025; mention only if it becomes part of the test plan.
